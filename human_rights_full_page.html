What Are Human Rights?

Human rights are the universal rights and freedoms entitled to every human being regardless of race, nationality, gender, or religion. They include the right to life, dignity, equality, thought, expression, and more.

A Brief History

Human rights evolved through struggle across centuries. One milestone was the Petition of Right (1628) which limited the monarchy's power and shaped early civil liberties.

Universal Declaration

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights affirms dignity, equality, justice and freedom for all humans. It outlines rights such as movement, expression, nationality, work, and education.

Lectures

Lecture 1: Definition of Human Rights

Human rights are the basic freedoms and entitlements that belong to every person simply for being human. These include the right to life, dignity, equality, freedom of expression, and protection from discrimination. They ensure that every individual can make life choices freely and live with respect.

Lecture 2: Historical Development of Human Rights

The evolution of human rights spans centuries. A major milestone was the Petition of Right (1628), issued by the English Parliament. It established key principles including no taxation without representation, no imprisonment without cause, and prohibiting forced housing of soldiers—laying early foundations for modern human rights standards.

Lecture 3: Key Articles from the Universal Declaration

  • Article 13: Freedom of movement and the right to leave and return to one’s country.
  • Article 15: The right to nationality and protection against arbitrary loss of citizenship.
  • Article 17: The right to own property individually or collectively.
  • Article 19: Freedom of opinion, expression, and access to information.
  • Article 20: Freedom of peaceful assembly and association.
  • Article 21: Political participation and free elections.
  • Article 23: The right to work, fair conditions, and equal pay.
  • Article 24: The right to rest and reasonable working hours.
  • Article 26: The right to education, especially free elementary education.
